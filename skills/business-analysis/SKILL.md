# Business Analysis

## Overview

Turn scattered information into a business judgment that is useful for decisions, not just summaries.
Prefer concrete conclusions, explicit assumptions, and comparable outputs over long generic analysis.

## Core Use Cases

- Judge whether a project, niche, or business model is worth doing
- Research a market and identify demand, competition, and positioning
- Analyze a competitor's offer, pricing, funnel, and likely revenue logic
- Compare several options and rank them for a specific founder or team
- Decide whether a business is worth imitating and which layer is actually copyable

## Operating Rules

- Start by identifying the decision being made. Do not analyze in the abstract.
- State what is known, what is inferred, and what is uncertain.
- Prefer first-order commercial questions: who pays, why they pay, how often they pay, how hard it is to win, and whether the model compounds.
- Do not confuse attention with business quality. Traffic, followers, and noise are only supporting signals.
- Always separate:
  - signal: evidence directly observed
  - inference: likely explanation from evidence
  - judgment: what to do next
- If the user is comparing several directions, normalize them against the same criteria.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the object of analysis

- What exactly is being judged:
  - market
  - business
  - product
  - offer
  - competitor
  - founder path
  - mimic opportunity
- What is the decision:
  - enter / not enter
  - rank options
  - copy / not copy
  - research further / ignore
  - use as reference only

### 2. Identify the business model

At minimum, answer:

- Who is the customer?
- What problem is being solved?
- What is being sold?
- How does money actually come in?
- Is the revenue one-time, recurring, service-based, transaction-based, or mixed?
- What part appears scalable and what part is still manual?

### 3. Judge the engine

Use these lenses:

- demand: is this a real painkiller or just interesting?
- monetization: is the payment path clear?
- delivery: how heavy is fulfillment?
- repeatability: can this be repeated without heroic effort?
- defensibility: what is hard to replace?
- compounding: do content, data, users, templates, or workflows accumulate?
- fit: is this suitable for the user's resources, background, and team size?

### 4. Decide what is copyable

Break the target into layers:

- audience layer
- content layer
- offer layer
- funnel layer
- ops layer
- distribution layer
- founder advantage layer

Do not recommend copying founder-specific moats unless the user already has equivalent assets.

### 5. Produce a decision-ready output

Default output sections:

1. One-line conclusion
2. Business model summary
3. Why customers pay
4. Revenue logic
5. Advantages and constraints
6. Risks and unknowns
7. What is worth copying
8. What is not worth copying
9. Final judgment
10. Next action

## Fast Heuristics

- High traffic + no clear offer usually means weak monetization clarity
- Small traffic + clear niche + clear paid product can still be a strong business
- Heavy service dependence lowers compounding unless systemized
- Subscription or repeat purchase improves model quality only if retention is plausible
- "Looks easy" often means the moat is hidden in distribution, taste, speed, or founder credibility

## What Good Analysis Looks Like

- Specific rather than inspirational
- Comparative rather than isolated
- Honest about uncertainty
- Actionable for the user's stage
- Focused on commercial reality, not trend language
